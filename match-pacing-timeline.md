# Match Pacing Timeline — The Commission (1v1, 40 Minutes)

## 1. Pacing Philosophy

The Commission targets the deliberate, escalating rhythm of Age of Empires II rather than the frantic, APM-intensive opening of StarCraft. The first few minutes are quiet and purposeful: players scout the map, claim neutral buildings, and establish their early economy through Speakeasies and racket income. Combat in this phase is rare and low-stakes — a scuffle over a contested block, not a game-ending rush. As players advance into Tier 2 and beyond, the decision space widens: military commitments become more expensive, territory becomes harder to hold, and every engagement carries real consequence. By the late game, the city is carved into warring empires, and battles are cinematic street wars fought with armored cars, enforcers, and explosives — the kind of moments players remember and talk about. The pacing ensures that early aggression is viable but not mandatory, defensive play is rewarded but not dominant, and every minute of the match presents a meaningful choice between expanding, fighting, and advancing up the tier ladder.

## 4. Decision Point Callouts

### Min ~5: First Contact — Fight or Flank?

Your scout Thugs have found the enemy near a contested neutral building in the center of the map. You have 4 Thug squads; so does your opponent. The building generates income and provides a garrison point, but fighting for it will cost you squads — and at 75$ each, replacing them delays your second Speakeasy. This is the match's first real decision: commit to the fight and try to claim center control (which pays dividends for the next 10 minutes), or concede the building and secure uncontested territory on the flanks. Aggressive players will invest the squads and accept short-term economic loss for positional advantage. Defensive players will take the safe route, knowing that a fast Tier 2 advance matters more than one building. Neither answer is wrong — but the consequences ripple through the entire mid-game.

### Min ~9: Tier 2 Timing — Advance or Punish?

Tier 2 costs roughly 300$ and takes 30–45 seconds to complete. During that time, you're not training units and your economy takes a hit. If your opponent advances first, you have a narrow window to punish them with a Tier 1 push — 6 Thug squads hitting their territory while they're vulnerable. But if you wait too long, their Enforcers arrive and your Thugs become outclassed. Conversely, advancing first gives you the quality advantage but risks losing territory if the opponent is aggressive. The optimal play depends on scouting: if you see the enemy advancing, you choose between mirroring them (safe, even) or punishing (risky, potentially game-winning). This is the AoE2 "Feudal timing" equivalent — one of the most-discussed decision points in competitive play.

### Min ~14: Logistics Commitment — Goods Pipeline or Military Pressure?

Tier 3 requires Goods — a secondary resource produced by Distilleries and transported through a supply chain. Building your first Distillery costs around 250$, and it takes 2–3 minutes before Goods start flowing meaningfully. That's 250$ not spent on Enforcers or vehicles, plus the opportunity cost of workers assigned to production instead of fighting. If you skip the Goods pipeline, you commit to winning with a Tier 2 army — more units now, but no path to Tier 3's powerful Capos and Armored Cars. This is the match's economic crossroads. Players who invest in Goods are betting on a 20+ minute game. Players who skip it are betting they can end it before Tier 3 matters. Reading your opponent's choice is critical: if they're building a Distillery and you're not, you need to attack before their investment pays off.

### Min ~18: Territory Peak — Push or Consolidate?

You control roughly 35–40% of the contestable map. Your opponent holds a similar amount. The remaining neutral territory is either in no-man's-land or hotly contested. Pushing beyond your current borders means fighting into the enemy's established positions — their garrisoned buildings, their Lookout Posts, their reinforcement lines. It's expensive in units and attention. But consolidating means ceding the initiative: your opponent gets to choose when and where the next fight happens. The decision often hinges on your strategic identity — are you the aggressor or the defender in this match? Aggressive players push now to deny the opponent economic parity. Defensive players upgrade their captured buildings, research technologies, and prepare for the Tier 3 power spike that will let them break through later with quality instead of quantity.

### Min ~22: Tier 3 Power Spike — Strike Now or Build Mass?

Tier 3 is online. You can train your first Capo squad — an elite infantry unit that beats anything Tier 2 can field in a straight fight. The temptation is to attack immediately and exploit the quality gap before your opponent reaches T3 themselves. But a single Capo squad isn't an army. Attacking with one elite unit and a supporting cast of T2 leftovers is risky; if you lose the Capo, you've wasted 180$ and your power spike. The alternative is patience: train 3–4 Capos, add an Armored Car, and attack with a cohesive T3 force at minute 24–25. The danger is that your opponent uses this time to reach T3 themselves, equalizing the quality gap. This decision rewards scouting — if you can confirm your opponent is still advancing, strike now. If they're already at T3, wait for critical mass.

### Min ~30: Endgame Choice — Tier 4 or All-In?

Tier 4 costs a fortune: roughly 800$ in Cash, a significant Goods stockpile, and Influence (a tertiary resource earned through map control and combat). Advancing to T4 means your army gets no reinforcements for nearly a minute, and your economy is gutted. But The Empire tier unlocks The Don, Tommy Gun squads, and victory condition buildings — it's the path to actually winning the game, not just fighting it. The alternative is going all-in with your current T3 army: spend every dollar on Capos and Armored Cars, and try to destroy the enemy before T4 matters. This is the most consequential decision in the match. If you advance to T4 and your opponent all-ins, you must survive the assault. If you all-in and your opponent reaches T4, their elite units will eventually overwhelm you. The correct choice depends on your position: if you're ahead, T4 is the safe win. If you're behind, all-in is your only chance.

### Min ~36: Victory Path — Commit or Pivot?

The match is in its final phase. You've been pushing toward a specific victory condition — perhaps capturing City Hall, perhaps reaching the Economic Dominance threshold, perhaps eliminating the enemy's key buildings. But your progress has stalled, or your opponent is countering effectively. Do you commit harder to your current path, pouring your remaining resources into the push? Or do you pivot to a different victory condition that your opponent isn't prepared for? Pivoting is expensive — you've already invested in one path — but a successful pivot catches the opponent off guard. At this stage of the match, both players are running low on resources and replacement units. Every squad lost is harder to replace, every building destroyed is a permanent economic wound. The player who reads the situation correctly and commits decisively — whether to their current path or a new one — wins the game. Hesitation is the only guaranteed loss.

## 5. Resource Benchmarks: Aggressive vs. Economic Build

This table compares two archetypal playstyles across the match. The "Aggressive" player prioritizes early military, fights for territory, and delays economic upgrades. The "Economic" player fast-expands, invests in Speakeasies and income upgrades, minimizes military spending, and rushes tier advancement. In practice, most players blend elements of both — these are the extremes.

**Key Takeaway:** The Aggressive build has a power spike between minutes 12–20, when military investment translates to territorial control and the Economic player hasn't yet reaped the benefits of their infrastructure. If the Aggressive player can force a decisive engagement in this window — destroying key buildings, cutting supply lines, or eliminating enough of the enemy army — they win. If the Economic player survives this window intact, their superior income, earlier tier advancement, and more efficient late-game production overwhelm the Aggressive player by minute 30+. Both builds are viable; the match is decided by whether the Aggressive player can convert their mid-game advantage into a knockout blow.
