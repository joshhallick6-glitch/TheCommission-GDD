# Unit & Vehicle Stat Sheets — The Commission

> PvP-first RTS. Prohibition-era crime city. CoH-style tactical urban combat with cover, suppression, garrisons, and flanking.

**Tier Progression:** Street Crew (T1) -> The Outfit (T2) -> The Syndicate (T3) -> The Empire (T4)

## 5. Design Notes

### Soft-Counter Philosophy

- **No hard immunities.** Every unit can damage every other unit. Even Street Thugs can chip down a War Wagon given enough time (they just shouldn't try). The counter system creates 2:1 to 3:1 effectiveness swings, not binary win/lose matchups.
- **Cost-efficiency is the real counter.** When Tommy Gunners are `++` vs Thugs, it means a $200 Tommy Gunner squad trades favorably against $150+ worth of Thugs. The Thugs still deal damage and might win with a 3:1 numbers advantage.
- **Positioning overrides counters.** A squad of Thugs in heavy cover behind sandbags can hold off Enforcers that would destroy them in the open. The counter matrix assumes equal positioning; terrain flips matchups.
- **Abilities are the tiebreaker.** Two equal-tier squads in neutral matchups come down to ability usage and micro. The player who times Rally, Spray and Pray, or Breach correctly wins the fight.

### Cover and Urban Terrain

- **Cover levels:** None (open street) -> Light (fences, crates, car wrecks) -> Heavy (sandbags, low walls, dumpsters) -> Garrison (inside buildings).
- **Cover modifiers:** Light cover grants 25% damage reduction. Heavy cover grants 50% damage reduction. Garrison grants 75% damage reduction + immunity to suppression (but vulnerability to Arsonists and Breach).
- **Directional cover.** Cover only protects against fire from the direction the cover faces. Flanking attacks bypass cover bonuses entirely, making positioning and map awareness critical.
- **Destructible cover.** Light cover is destroyed by vehicle rams, explosions, and sustained fire. Heavy cover takes longer but can be destroyed. Garrisonable buildings can be structurally damaged (reduced cover bonus) or collapsed entirely at high damage thresholds.
- **Urban chokepoints.** The city map is designed with alleys, intersections, and building clusters that create natural killzones. Controlling sight lines and cover positions is as important as unit composition.

### Why Vehicles Are Strong But Not Dominant

- **Rock-paper-scissors with infantry.** Vehicles crush infantry in the open but are vulnerable to Saboteurs (demo charges deal 500 flat damage, enough to one-shot Light armor vehicles), Arsonists (sustained fire damage ignores armor), and massed close-range infantry (Enforcers with shotguns).
- **Urban terrain limits vehicles.** Narrow alleys block vehicle movement. Buildings create ambush points. Vehicles cannot enter buildings or use cover. A Machine Gun Car dominating an open intersection is lethal; the same car caught in a dead-end alley by two Saboteur squads is scrap metal.
- **Goods cost is a limiter.** Vehicles cost both Cash and Goods, and Goods require Racket income and transport logistics. Spamming vehicles is not economically viable in the early-mid game. A player who over-invests in vehicles has fewer infantry to hold territory and protect their economy.
- **No vehicle repair in the field (without Wheelman).** Damaged vehicles stay damaged unless returned to the Garage or accompanied by a Wheelman. This creates attrition pressure; vehicles that survive a fight at 30% HP are liabilities until repaired.
- **Crew vulnerability.** If the driver is killed (rare, requires Hitman Mark for Death or high-damage burst), the vehicle is disabled until a new crew is assigned. This creates counterplay beyond raw DPS.

### Suppression Mechanics

- **What suppression does.** Suppressed squads suffer -50% movement speed, -25% accuracy, and cannot use active abilities. Heavily suppressed squads are "pinned" — they cannot move at all and suffer -50% accuracy (but can still fire).
- **How suppression is applied.** Units with the Suppression trait (Tommy Gunners, mounted MGs, War Wagon) generate suppression points on enemy squads they are firing at. Accumulating 50 suppression points triggers "Suppressed" status. 100 points triggers "Pinned."
- **Suppression decay.** Suppression points decay at 10/sec when the squad is not being fired upon. Moving into heavy cover accelerates decay to 20/sec. Breaking line of sight instantly halves current suppression points.
- **Counter-suppression play.** Garrisoned units are immune to suppression. Flanking a suppressing unit breaks the suppression angle (suppression only applies in the firer's forward arc). Smoke grenades (planned Saboteur T3 upgrade) block suppression line of sight. The Consigliere's morale heal does not remove suppression but prevents the morale spiral that suppression can trigger.
- **Design intent.** Suppression rewards fire superiority and punishes blobbing (clumping squads together). It creates a "pin and flank" meta where one squad pins the enemy and another squad moves to a flanking position to deliver the killing blow. This mirrors real combined-arms tactics and creates skill expression in micro.

### Squad Wipe vs Retreat Mechanics

- **Squads are not binary.** A 4-man squad at 1 member is still alive and still has veterancy. Preserving even a single squad member allows the unit to be reinforced back to full strength at a Hideout or Safehouse.
- **Retreat command.** Any squad can retreat at any time. Retreating squads move at 1.5x normal speed toward the nearest friendly Hideout/Safehouse. They cannot attack or use abilities while retreating but gain +25% damage resistance (running with their heads down).
- **Retreat is not free.** Retreating squads still take fire. If the retreat path goes through enemy-controlled territory, the squad may be wiped before reaching safety. This makes map control and territory directly relevant to attrition warfare.
- **Veterancy is the reward for retreating.** Squads gain veterancy through combat (kills, objectives, surviving engagements). Veteran squads receive permanent stat bonuses (Vet 1: +10% accuracy, Vet 2: +15% damage resistance, Vet 3: +unique ability upgrade). Losing a Vet 3 squad is a catastrophic loss that cannot be replaced by simply training a new one.
- **Squad wipe penalty.** If all members of a squad die, the squad is permanently lost. The player also suffers a small morale penalty to all nearby squads (witnessing allies wiped out is demoralizing). This creates a risk/reward tension: do you retreat a badly damaged squad and fight undermanned, or do you push and risk a full wipe?
- **Design intent.** The retreat system rewards players who manage their army carefully over those who throw units away. It creates a "bleeding" playstyle where both players are gradually wearing each other down, with decisive moments (squad wipes, successful retreats under fire) determining momentum shifts. The veterancy system ensures that experienced squads are worth more than fresh replacements, making army preservation a core skill.
