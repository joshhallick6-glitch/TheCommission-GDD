# Victory Condition Comparison Matrix — The Commission

## Design Intent

Multiple victory conditions are the backbone of strategic depth in The Commission. By offering four distinct paths to victory — military, territorial, political, and economic — the game prevents any single dominant strategy from calcifying the meta. Players must constantly read their opponent's intent and adapt, because every victory path has clear, actionable counterplay. A player who tunnels on one win condition becomes predictable and vulnerable; a player who stays flexible and threatens multiple paths simultaneously forces difficult decisions from their opponent. This tension — between committing to a plan and staying adaptable — is what drives replayability and competitive skill expression across the full 40-minute match arc.

## Victory Path Profiles

### Elimination

> *"There's only one way to be sure — burn it all down."*

**Mechanics:** Elimination requires the complete destruction of every enemy Compound and Safehouse on the map. There is no countdown, no threshold, and no shortcut — you must find and raze every last structure. If the opponent has hidden Safehouses in obscure corners of the city, you must hunt them down.

**Strategic Identity:** Rewards aggressive, macro-heavy players who excel at army composition, sustained production, and decisive engagement timing. This is the path for players who want to win through superior force.

**Strengths:**
- Always available regardless of tier, map state, or game mode — cannot be "turned off" by rule variants.
- The most direct path: if you are winning militarily, nothing can stop you except a better army.
- Forces the opponent to invest in defense, which slows their own win conditions.

**Weaknesses:**
- Highest difficulty — requires dominant military position across the entire map, not just one front.
- Extremely expensive in total resources; prolonged fighting drains all three resource types.
- Highly visible — the opponent always knows when you're pushing for Elimination and can prepare accordingly.

**Key Buildings/Units:** Barracks and armories at all tiers, vehicle depots (Tier 3+), siege-capable units (demolition crews, artillery cars), forward Safehouses for reinforcement.

**Telltale Signs:** Opponent is investing heavily in combat units with minimal economic diversification. Multiple Safehouses pushed aggressively forward. Scouting activity probing for your hidden structures.

**Common Pivots:** If a full Elimination push stalls, players often pivot to Monopoly by leveraging their existing army to seize and hold Turf Points. The military infrastructure translates directly into territorial control.

---

### Monopoly

> *"Every block, every corner, every business — this is my city now."*

**Mechanics:** The map contains a set number of designated Turf Points — key intersections, landmark businesses, and strategic locations. When a player controls 70% or more of these points, a visible 5-minute countdown begins. If the player maintains 70% control for the full 5 minutes without dropping below the threshold at any point, they win. The countdown resets entirely if control dips below 70%, even momentarily.

**Strategic Identity:** Rewards map-aware, multitasking players who excel at distributing forces, reading opponent rotations, and maintaining pressure across multiple fronts simultaneously. This is the path for players who win through superior positioning.

**Strengths:**
- Earliest achievable victory condition — a strong Tier 2 push can trigger the countdown before opponents reach Tier 3.
- Controlling the map generates ongoing economic and Influence benefits, making Monopoly self-reinforcing.
- Forces the opponent into a reactive posture, running around the map contesting points rather than executing their own strategy.

**Weaknesses:**
- Very high visibility — the opponent sees exactly which points you hold, knows the countdown timer, and can focus counterattacks precisely.
- Requires spreading forces thin across the entire map; concentrated enemy strikes can break the threshold with a single well-placed attack.
- The 5-minute hold requirement means you must sustain dominance, not just achieve a momentary spike — one lost firefight can reset the clock.

**Key Buildings/Units:** Garrison structures at Turf Points, mobile response squads, defensive emplacements (sandbags, roadblocks), scout cars for rapid redeployment, Safehouses positioned near Turf Point clusters.

**Telltale Signs:** Opponent is aggressively capturing neutral Turf Points in the early-mid game. Garrison buildings appearing at captured locations. Forces spread across the map in small defensive groups rather than concentrated in one army.

**Common Pivots:** If the opponent consistently breaks the countdown, Monopoly players pivot to Commission Vote (their territorial holdings already generate Influence) or to Elimination (their distributed forces can converge for a concentrated strike on the now-weakened opponent).

### The Commission Vote

> *"Why fight for the city when the city can choose you?"*

**Mechanics:** Influence is accumulated through political connections, community control, and criminal prestige — generated by specific buildings, rackets, and actions throughout the game. When a player reaches 500 Influence, a 3-minute countdown begins, representing the ruling Commission deliberating and preparing to formally recognize the player's family as dominant. The countdown is broadcast to all players. If the player's Influence drops below 500 during the countdown (through sabotage, building destruction, or opponent interference), the countdown pauses until 500 is re-achieved.

**Strategic Identity:** Rewards patient, infrastructure-focused players who build layered economies and protect key assets. This is the path for players who win through superior planning and resource management — the "wonder victory" archetype adapted for a crime setting.

**Strengths:**
- Semi-visible — while the Influence score is public, the exact sources and timeline are harder for opponents to read than Monopoly's map presence.
- Synergizes naturally with Monopoly, creating powerful dual-threat positions.
- The 3-minute countdown is shorter than Monopoly's 5-minute hold, making it harder to counter once triggered.

**Weaknesses:**
- Requires Tier 3+ infrastructure, meaning it cannot be rushed in the early game.
- Influence-generating buildings are high-value targets; losing them is devastating and recovery is slow.
- Opponents who recognize the Commission Vote threat can focus sabotage efforts specifically on Influence sources.

**Key Buildings/Units:** Political offices, community centers, high-end rackets (casinos, speakeasies), churches/charities (legitimacy fronts), informant networks, defensive units to protect infrastructure.

**Telltale Signs:** Opponent building multiple Influence-generating structures rather than military production. Investment in Tier 3 infrastructure earlier than expected. Defensive posture with fortified positions around key buildings rather than aggressive expansion.

**Common Pivots:** If Influence buildings are destroyed, Commission Vote players often pivot to Hostile Takeover (their intact economic base can be redirected toward Cash accumulation) or to Monopoly (their territorial holdings from Influence generation can be leveraged for point control).

### Hostile Takeover

> *"Everyone has a price — and I can afford all of them."*

**Mechanics:** At any point after reaching Tier 3, a player can initiate a Hostile Takeover by spending 5,000 Cash in a single lump payment at their Compound. This represents the player's family buying out every judge, politician, police chief, and rival underboss in the city through pure financial leverage. The payment is instant — once initiated, the game ends immediately with no countdown. However, the player must physically have 5,000 Cash stockpiled and must execute the payment at their Compound, meaning the Compound must be standing and accessible.

**Strategic Identity:** Rewards economically dominant players who can build and protect a massive Cash reserve while fending off opponents. This is the path for players who win through superior economic play and deception — the "stealth victory" that opponents must anticipate rather than react to.

**Strengths:**
- No countdown — once you have the Cash and execute the payment, you win instantly with no window for counterplay.
- Hidden — opponents cannot see your Cash total and may not realize you are close to 5,000 until it is too late.
- Punishes opponents who ignore your economy and focus purely on military or territorial play.

**Weaknesses:**
- Extremely expensive — 5,000 Cash is a massive sum that requires sustained economic dominance and minimal military spending.
- Requires your Compound to be standing, making you vulnerable to targeted strikes on your most important structure.
- Any forced military expenditure (defending raids, replacing lost units) directly delays the victory by draining your Cash reserves.

**Key Buildings/Units:** Banks, warehouses, smuggling operations, high-revenue businesses, Compound fortifications, a lean defensive military focused on protecting economic assets rather than projecting power.

**Telltale Signs:** Opponent has a booming economy but suspiciously few military units. Compound is heavily fortified beyond normal levels. The opponent avoids fights they could win, suggesting they are preserving Cash rather than spending it. Late-game passivity from a player who clearly has the resources to be aggressive.

**Common Pivots:** If the economy is disrupted and 5,000 Cash becomes unreachable, Hostile Takeover players pivot to Commission Vote (their economic infrastructure can be partially redirected toward Influence generation) or simply invest their remaining Cash into a military push for Elimination while they still have a resource advantage.

## Competitive Mode Notes

All four victory conditions should remain active in ranked and tournament play. Removing any condition would reduce strategic depth and narrow the meta toward the remaining options — exactly the problem multiple victory conditions are designed to solve. However, two adjustments are worth considering for competitive integrity. First, Hostile Takeover's hidden nature means opponents have no information to act on; adding a subtle tell — such as a brief, non-specific "economic milestone" notification when a player crosses 3,500 Cash — would give attentive opponents a window to respond without fully revealing the strategy. Second, the Monopoly countdown duration could be reduced from 5 minutes to 4 minutes in 1v1 ranked play, since the 5-minute hold can feel punishing in smaller maps where the defender has less ground to cover when contesting. In team formats (2v2+), all values should remain as designed, since coordinated counterplay makes every victory condition harder to achieve and the longer timers are necessary to prevent snowball victories.
